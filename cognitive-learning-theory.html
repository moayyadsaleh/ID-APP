6. Cognitive Learning Theory
It focuses on the internal processes of learning, emphasizing the role of mental activities such as thinking, memory, and problem-solving in acquiring knowledge. It posits that instruction should focus on how the mind processes information.

Key principles of Cognitive Learning Theory: Active Engagement/learning, Prior Knowledge, Scaffolding, Cognitive Load, Concept Mapping, Memory Techniques, Metacognition, Meaningful Context, Feedback and Practice

Cognitive Load Theory: Cognitive Load Theory is all about how our brain handles information. It suggests that our working memory has a limited capacity, so if too much information is presented at once, it can overload our brain, making learning less effective. In instructional design, we use this theory to design materials that don't overwhelm learners, by breaking content into smaller chunks, using clear instructions, and providing visuals to aid understanding.

Example course: To apply Cognitive Learning Theory in a course, I would create an interactive and learner-centered environment that encourages active engagement. I would begin by activating prior knowledge through pre-assessment or discussions to help learners connect new content to what they already know. Scaffolding techniques would be employed, gradually reducing support as learners become more independent. To manage cognitive load, complex information would be broken into smaller, digestible parts, and I would include visual aids like concept maps. Regular feedback, practice, and the use of memory techniques such as mnemonic devices and spaced repetition would reinforce learning. I would also promote metacognitive skills by encouraging learners to reflect on their learning process and incorporate real-world examples to provide a meaningful context. Activities like case studies and debates would help develop critical thinking and problem-solving abilities, applying the theory in practical, reflective ways.
