Dick and Carey Model

The Dick and Carey Model is a systematic approach to instructional design that guides designers through a structured sequence of steps to create effective learning experiences. Unlike linear models, the Dick and Carey approach views instructional design as an interconnected system, where each component (like objectives, assessments, and materials) impacts the others. This model is especially valuable for instructional designers aiming to produce measurable, goal-oriented learning outcomes.

By following this model, designers can create instructional materials that are not only engaging but also meet specific performance standards. It is particularly useful when designing materials for complex learning tasks or in settings where performance outcomes must be precise and measurable.

When to Use the Dick and Carey Model: This model is ideal for instructional projects that require in-depth planning and structured evaluation, such as corporate training, academic courses, and large-scale learning programs. It is most effective when designing for environments where feedback and iterative improvement are possible, as the model includes formative evaluation steps. By using the Dick and Carey Model, instructional designers can ensure that each phase of their work builds on the last, creating a cohesive and impactful learning experience.

1. Identify Instructional Goals
The first step is to establish clear, measurable goals for what learners should achieve by the end of the instructional process. These goals should reflect the skills, knowledge, or behaviors learners need to develop. Setting well-defined goals helps to shape the entire instructional design process and ensures that all subsequent steps are focused on achieving these outcomes.

2. Conduct Instructional Analysis
This step involves breaking down the instructional goals into specific tasks or components that learners need to master. By analyzing the steps required to reach each goal, instructional designers can identify the foundational skills and knowledge that will support learning. This phase often includes task analysis or concept mapping, providing a roadmap for content development.

3. Analyze Learners and Context
Here, designers assess the characteristics of their target audience and the environment in which the learning will occur. Factors like learners' prior knowledge, motivation, and cultural background can influence how materials are developed. Additionally, understanding the learning environment (e.g., online, classroom, blended) ensures that instructional strategies are relevant and accessible.

4. Write Performance Objectives
Based on the instructional goals and analysis, specific performance objectives are developed. These objectives clearly state what learners should be able to do upon completion, the conditions under which they should perform, and the criteria for successful performance. Performance objectives serve as the foundation for both instructional activities and assessments, ensuring that learning is purposeful and measurable.

5. Develop Assessment Instruments
Assessment instruments are created to evaluate whether learners meet the performance objectives. These assessments can be formative (ongoing checks for understanding) or summative (final evaluations). Effective assessments align directly with the objectives and help instructional designers gauge the success of their materials.

6. Develop Instructional Strategy
In this phase, designers determine the instructional approach and activities that will support learning. This could include lectures, discussions, hands-on exercises, multimedia elements, or interactive components. The instructional strategy should be tailored to the learning objectives, audience needs, and learning environment to maximize engagement and effectiveness.

7. Develop and Select Instructional Materials
After defining the strategy, instructional materials are created or selected. These materials can include digital content, handouts, presentations, videos, and more. Materials should support the instructional strategy and be directly aligned with the performance objectives to ensure a cohesive learning experience.

8. Design and Conduct Formative Evaluation
Formative evaluation allows designers to test the instructional materials in phases (e.g., through one-on-one testing, small group trials, or field trials). Feedback gathered during these evaluations helps identify areas for improvement, enabling revisions before the final rollout. This iterative process ensures that materials are refined and optimized for effectiveness.

9. Revise Instruction
Based on the formative evaluation feedback, instructional materials are adjusted to better meet learning objectives and address any issues identified. Revision is an essential part of this model, as it allows designers to make data-driven improvements to the content, structure, and delivery method, ensuring the final product is as effective as possible.

10. Design and Conduct Summative Evaluation
The final phase involves a comprehensive evaluation of the instructional program's effectiveness after it has been fully implemented. Summative evaluation assesses whether the instructional goals were achieved and identifies the impact on learner performance. This evaluation provides valuable insights for future projects and can be used to demonstrate the value of the instructional materials.
